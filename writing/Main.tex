  \begin{abstract}
  \noindent As states increasingly govern through digital platforms, how citizens engage with official social media remains poorly understood. Official accounts combine public-service provision with propaganda, making interaction with them both information use and a politically consequential act visible to the state and other users. Using 4.21 million posts from 329 Chinese local-government WeChat accounts and a regression discontinuity design around two platform reforms, I show that the public visibility of interaction shapes participation. Making affirmative responses more publicly visible reduced approval-oriented engagement by 1.9 percentage points; restoring a less visible option increased it by 2.9 points and shifted participation away from public endorsement. Visible-endorsement avoidance is strongest for hard propaganda and remains higher for soft propaganda than for public-service content. Platform design thus structures political participation by changing the visibility of expression, with implications for the study of citizen-state interaction under digital governance.

\vspace{0.2in}

\noindent \textbf{Keywords}: Digital Authoritarianism; Preference Falsification; Propaganda; Social Media; State--Society Interaction.

 \end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

How do citizens in autocracies interact with official social media, and what do those traces actually mean? As propaganda, public communication, and service delivery migrate onto digital platforms, state-controlled accounts accumulate vast numbers of reads, likes, and shares. These metrics are tempting to interpret as indicators of propaganda success or regime support. Yet in authoritarian settings, interacting with official content is rarely a purely expressive act. Citizens may engage because they need information, because a post is difficult to avoid, because approval is low-cost, or because visible endorsement carries reputational and political risk. The political meaning of digital engagement is therefore fundamentally ambiguous.

Existing work speaks to pieces of this puzzle, but rarely to the mechanism at stake here. Research on Chinese propaganda shows that the party-state competes for attention through commercialization, soft propaganda, distraction, and popularization rather than relying on crude indoctrination alone \parencite{Brady2008,KingPanRoberts2017,LuPan2021,MattinglyYao2022,RepnikovaFang2018,Roberts2018,Stockmann2013,Wang2024Propaganda,Zou2021}. Research on preference falsification and self-censorship emphasizes the gap between private belief and public expression under pressure \parencite{CarterCarterSchick2024,JiangYang2016,Kuran1995,NoelleNeumann1993,ShenTruex2020}. A third literature shows that government WeChat accounts are not merely propaganda outlets: local authorities also use them for public service delivery, routine contact, and audience acquisition \parencite{JiangEtAl2021,LiuMaXia2024,YuShen2026,Zheng2013,ZhengZheng2014}. A related strand highlights semi-official accounts, delegated censorship, and layered platform governance, suggesting that state influence is often embedded in organizationally ambiguous or platform-native forms \parencite{Neagli2021,SunZhao2022,TaiFu2020}. What these literatures do not yet explain well is how platform design changes the political meaning of interaction itself.

I argue that official social media in digital authoritarianism are mixed channels that bundle public service information with political messaging, and that citizens sort their behavior across different levels of expressive risk. On WeChat, reading is low-risk consumption, a like is low-visibility approval, \textit{zaikan} is socially legible endorsement, and sharing is the highest-visibility form of amplification. This layered-expression perspective shifts the theoretical question away from whether citizens ``believe'' propaganda and toward how they manage observable traces of alignment.

I evaluate this argument using 4.21 million post titles published by 329 prefecture-level government WeChat official accounts between 2015 and 2024. The article makes two empirical moves. First, I use a theory-driven `LLM + human' classification workflow to assign fine-grained post labels and then collapse them into three main content families: hard propaganda, soft propaganda, and public service. Second, I use the 2018 and 2020 WeChat interface reforms to motivate a redesigned empirical analysis of how platform changes alter the meaning of a click.

This article makes three contributions. First, it extends preference falsification theory from speech to platform-mediated behavior by showing that digital expression is layered across reading, liking, \textit{zaikan}, and sharing. Second, it contributes to the study of propaganda and media consumption by separating attention from endorsement and by showing how service content both builds and competes for political attention. Third, it recasts official social media as an everyday site of authoritarian state--society interaction, where governments manage not only information but also the terms under which citizens can safely engage.

\section{Theory and Hypotheses}

Official social media in authoritarian settings should be understood as a platformed interaction regime rather than as a simple channel of state persuasion. In classic preference falsification accounts, the central distinction is between private preference and public statement. On platforms such as WeChat, however, expression is not binary. The interface decomposes response into multiple actions that expose users to different audiences and therefore to different expressive risks. The state may observe aggregate traces, but friends, colleagues, and acquaintances can also infer meaning from visible acts. Digital preference falsification is therefore layered: the same user may privately consume official content, register low-visibility approval, and avoid socially visible endorsement \parencite{Kuran1995,NoelleNeumann1993,ShenTruex2020,TsaiMen2018,Xu2022WeChat}.

This layered-expression logic matters because Chinese government WeChat accounts are mixed channels rather than purely political outlets. Existing research shows that state agencies use social media not only to disseminate political messages but also to provide services, route transactions, manage crises, and sustain routine interaction with the public \parencite{JiangEtAl2021,LiuMaXia2024,YuShen2026,Zheng2013,ZhengZheng2014}. That literature is especially useful because it shows that many users approach official accounts as lookup tools or service interfaces rather than as ideological venues. Service content therefore helps governments build durable audiences, normalize repeated contact, and increase the probability that users encounter future political content. At the moment of consumption, however, service information also competes with overt political messaging for scarce attention. This is the paper's attention dilemma.

The same logic also changes how we should think about propaganda. Contemporary influence online often works through participatory persuasion, semi-official intermediaries, and delegated forms of control rather than through a single visible propaganda apparatus \parencite{KingPanRoberts2017,LuPan2021,Neagli2021,RepnikovaFang2018,SunZhao2022}. Hard propaganda foregrounds hierarchy, ideology, discipline, and coercive state capacity. Soft propaganda packages regime-favorable messages in more appealing, emotionally resonant, or developmental frames \parencite{MattinglyYao2022,PanShaoXu2022,Wang2024Propaganda}. Citizens may read all three kinds of content, but the willingness to leave an observable trace should vary with both content risk and interface design. Platform reforms do not merely change aesthetics. They reprice the political and social costs of interacting with the state.

This framework yields four expectations:

\noindent \textbf{H1 (Attention and Utility).} Public service content should attract more routine consumption than either hard or soft propaganda because it provides immediate utility and lower expressive stakes.

\noindent \textbf{H2 (Rebundling Visible Approval).} When the 2018 redesign bundles approval with public recommendation, low-visibility like responses should collapse and observed reactions should be reallocated into more socially legible channels.

\noindent \textbf{H3 (Private Substitution).} When low-visibility likes return in 2020, approval should shift back toward likes rather than rising uniformly across all public-facing reactions.

\noindent \textbf{H4 (Political-Risk Gradient).} These reallocations should be strongest for hard propaganda, then soft propaganda, and weakest for routine public-service content.

\section{Setting, Data, and Measurement}

WeChat is the central digital infrastructure of everyday life in China. Since its launch in 2011, it has evolved into a multipurpose ``super app'' that combines messaging, payments, news distribution, and social networking within a single platform. By the 2020s, it had become the primary interface through which Chinese citizens communicate, consume news, and access digital public and private services. For a study of official digital communication in China, WeChat matters not because it is just another social media platform, but because it is deeply embedded in ordinary social, administrative, and economic life \parencite{Ng2015,Roberts2020}.

Within this ecosystem, ``public accounts'' operate as mixed institutional channels. They transmit propaganda and political messaging, publicize leadership priorities, and provide highly practical information and service announcements \parencite{JiangEtAl2021,LiuMaXia2024,YuShen2026,Zheng2013,ZhengZheng2014}. At the same time, the broader WeChat ecosystem includes semi-official and organizationally ambiguous accounts that can carry state-aligned messages without looking fully official \parencite{Neagli2021}. A typical article displays a read counter together with several engagement mechanisms: a like button, \textit{haokan}/\textit{zaikan}, sharing, and collecting. These buttons do not measure the same thing with different labels. They ask users to leave different kinds of traces under different conditions of visibility.

The key institutional changes are the 2018 and 2020 interface reforms. In December 2018, WeChat replaced the original like button on newly pushed public-account articles with a ``good-looking'' button tightly integrated with the ``See'' tab. A previously quasi-private like was transformed into a socially legible recommendation. In June 2020, WeChat restored the traditional like button alongside \textit{zaikan} and sharing, thereby unbundling low-visibility approval from public recommendation. I treat 2017 as background discovery reform and 2018/2020 as the two main discontinuities for expressive engagement. Table~\ref{tab:policy_timepoints} summarizes these shifts.

This study uses the universe of posts published by 329 prefecture-level government WeChat public accounts between 1 January 2015 and 31 December 2024, covering 31 provincial-level units and 4,207,072 posts. The unit of observation for classification is the individual post title. For each post, I record the account identifier, publication date, title, and engagement indicators displayed in the interface, including reads, likes, shares, collecting, and \textit{haokan}/\textit{zaikan}. The archived source file also preserves a first-pass category field, a short reason field, a keyword field, and a confidence score from an earlier coarse classification pass; these metadata are used only for title-based normalization and coding, not as outcomes. Because WeChat top-codes some counters at 100,000 (displayed as ``100k+''), I recode those values to 100,001 so they remain ordered while preserving censoring at the upper bound.

To categorize post content at scale, I use an LLM as a text coder rather than as a generator. The production prompt is set before full-corpus assignment and uses only title-side inputs: title, account name, province, city, publication date, keywords, and the short reason field. Engagement outcomes, account-level outcome patterns, and reform-period indicators never enter the classification prompt. The coding workflow is built on a fixed ten-category taxonomy. The main text relies on this workflow only for measurement credibility; the SI documents taxonomy construction, coder-audit rules, and validation diagnostics.

I validate the LLM labels against a dual-coded gold-standard sample of 2,000 post titles coded under the same codebook and adjudicated to a final reference label. The SI reports the reliability protocol, aggregate validation metrics, class-wise performance, and a family-level confusion matrix. For the main analyses, I collapse the fine-grained labels into three theoretically motivated families: hard propaganda, soft propaganda, and public service. Hard propaganda includes overtly political and disciplinary content; soft propaganda packages regime-favorable messages through development, culture, tourism, and performance narratives; public service covers instrumental governance content such as procedures, welfare, health, traffic, weather, and emergency guidance. State-governance categories remain in the data for balance checks and appendix material but are not the paper's main theoretical families.

The paper's measurement logic is therefore theory-driven rather than purely unsupervised. The LLM solves the assignment problem at the post level; it does not decide which families matter theoretically. That distinction is important because the theoretical categories come from prior literature on mixed governance communication, soft propaganda, and expressive visibility rather than from an inductive topic model alone.

\section{Research Design}

The paper exploits two sharp interface discontinuities as natural experiments. On December 21, 2018, WeChat 7.0 replaced the article-bottom ``like'' button with ``good-looking'' (\textit{haokan}), bundling low-visibility approval with socially legible recommendation. On July 1, 2020, the ``like'' button was restored alongside \textit{zaikan} and a new ``share'' button, unbundling the two channels. Both changes were platform-wide, simultaneous, and exogenous to individual account or article characteristics.

I estimate sharp regression discontinuity designs centered on each cutoff. The running variable is days relative to the reform date, and the unit of observation is the individual article. All specifications use local linear polynomial estimation with a triangular kernel, MSE-optimal bandwidth selection, and robust bias-corrected confidence intervals following \textcite{CalonicoEtAl2014}. Standard errors are clustered at the account level (329 prefecture-level accounts) to account for within-account correlation.

Because the running variable is discrete at the day level and the article volume is highly uneven across accounts, the SI re-estimates the main specifications on account-day aggregates so that prolific posting accounts do not dominate the local fit. I also treat the 2017 discovery reform as a contrast case rather than a third main design because it broadened content discovery without directly rebundling the article-footer approval channel; SI~\ref{tab:rdd-2017-contrast} reports those estimates.

\section{Causal Findings}

\subsection{H2: Raising Visibility Reduces One-Click Engagement (2018)}

Table~\ref{tab:rdd-2018-main} reports RDD estimates at the December 2018 cutoff. The main outcome is the \textit{one-click engagement rate}, defined as the sum of like and haokan/zaikan rates, which captures the same footer-button slot across regimes.

The function of this table is formal identification: it provides the paper's main estimate of what happens when low-visibility approval is rebundled into a more public signal.

\input{../tables/rdd_2018_main.tex}

Importantly, the share rate shows no discontinuity at this cutoff ($p = 0.945$), confirming that the effect is specific to the one-click footer button.

\subsection{H4: Political-Risk Gradient}

Table~\ref{tab:rdd-2020-heterogeneity} reports heterogeneous RDD estimates at the 2020 cutoff by content family. The theoretical prediction is that reallocation from high-visibility zaikan to low-visibility like should be strongest for politically risky content.

Its function is to connect the interface argument back to political content. If visibility costs matter politically rather than mechanically, the size of the shift should vary systematically with content risk.

\input{../tables/rdd_2020_heterogeneity.tex}

The results confirm the gradient. The zaikan drop is largest for hard propaganda ($-0.017$, $p < 0.001$), followed by soft propaganda ($-0.014$, $p < 0.001$) and state governance ($-0.009$, $p = 0.006$). For public service content, the zaikan drop is small and not statistically significant ($-0.005$, $p = 0.306$). This ordering is consistent with the theory: citizens are most reluctant to leave socially visible endorsement traces on politically charged content, and most willing to shift approval to the restored low-visibility channel when the expressive risk is highest.

Figure~\ref{fig:rdd-2020-family} visualizes the 2020 unbundling separately by content family. The visible zaikan component remains especially compressed for hard and soft propaganda, while public service retains a larger socially legible component. That pattern sharpens the interpretation of H4: what varies across families is not simply total engagement, but the willingness to leave behind a visible endorsement trace.

The figure's function is interpretive. It shows why the family-specific table matters substantively: the core difference across families lies in the composition of approval between visible and less-visible traces, not just in overall clicking.

\subsection{Mechanism: Behavioral Self-Censorship and Visibility Avoidance}

The 2020 redesign makes it possible to quantify the mechanism more directly. The main 2020 table uses outcome-specific MSE-optimal bandwidths, which is appropriate for separate estimation but not for an exact decomposition. To recover an additive decomposition, I re-estimate the July 2020 cutoff with a \textit{shared} local-linear bandwidth inherited from the total one-click specification. Let $\hat{\mu}_{m}^{-}(h)$ and $\hat{\mu}_{m}^{+}(h)$ denote the left- and right-limit fitted values at the cutoff for outcome $m \in \{O, L, Z\}$, where $O$ is total one-click engagement, $L$ is like, and $Z$ is \textit{zaikan}. Using the common bandwidth $h$, I proceed in three steps: estimate the same local-linear specification for all three outcomes, define the shared-bandwidth jump as
\[
\hat{\tau}_{m}(h) = \hat{\mu}_{m}^{+}(h) - \hat{\mu}_{m}^{-}(h),
\]
and then convert those three jumps into descriptive mechanism summaries. Because $O = L + Z$ at the article level and all three outcomes are estimated with the same bandwidth, kernel, and local polynomial order, the decomposition satisfies
\[
\hat{\tau}_{O}(h) = \hat{\tau}_{L}(h) + \hat{\tau}_{Z}(h).
\]

I summarize the decomposition with two quantities. First, I define a \textit{behavioral self-censorship proxy} as the share of the post-2020 increase in low-visibility likes that shows up as net-new one-click expression rather than mere substitution away from visible endorsement:
\[
\text{Behavioral self-censorship proxy} = \frac{\hat{\tau}_{O}(h)}{\hat{\tau}_{L}(h)},
\qquad
\text{Visible-to-private substitution share} = \frac{-\hat{\tau}_{Z}(h)}{\hat{\tau}_{L}(h)}.
\]
Second, for each content family $f$, I define a \textit{visibility-avoidance index} (VAI) as
\[
\widehat{VAI}_{f} = \frac{\hat{\mu}_{L,f}^{+}(h)}
{\hat{\mu}_{L,f}^{+}(h) + \hat{\mu}_{Z,f}^{+}(h)}.
\]
This is the share of post-reform one-click approval routed through the lower-visibility like channel rather than publicly legible \textit{zaikan}. Higher values indicate stronger avoidance of visible endorsement conditional on leaving any one-click trace. I treat these quantities as descriptive mechanism summaries rather than as a true preference-falsification rate.

Table~\ref{tab:mechanism-decomposition} reports the aggregate decomposition. With the shared bandwidth, the one-click jump is 3.05 percentage points, the like jump is 4.29 points, and the \textit{zaikan} jump is $-1.24$ points, so the additive identity holds exactly. The behavioral self-censorship proxy is 71.0\%, meaning that about seven-tenths of the restored low-visibility approval appears as net-new one-click expression rather than simple migration out of visible \textit{zaikan}. The remaining 29.0\% is visible-to-private substitution. The post-cutoff VAI is 68.3\%, indicating that more than two-thirds of one-click approval at the cutoff is routed through the less visible channel.

Its function is mechanism quantification. Instead of stopping at the statement that likes rise and \textit{zaikan} falls, the table translates the 2020 redesign into a lower-claim but directly interpretable behavioral proxy for self-censorship and visible-trace avoidance.

Public service has the lowest VAI at 52.7\%, meaning approval remains relatively balanced between low- and high-visibility channels. Hard propaganda has by far the highest VAI at 88.2\%, implying that nearly nine in ten one-click approval traces are routed through likes rather than \textit{zaikan}. State governance and soft propaganda lie in between, both well above public service. This pattern is exactly what the visibility-cost argument predicts: once content becomes politically riskier, users who still engage disproportionately choose the less visible channel.

Their function is to make the risk gradient legible in a single mechanism metric. \section{Conclusion}

Official engagement metrics on authoritarian platforms are not transparent measures of support. They are shaped by mixed content environments and by the visibility structure of the interface itself. On WeChat, the central analytical task is therefore to separate attention from endorsement and to study how interface design conditions the forms of engagement that users are willing to leave behind.

The broader implication is that digital authoritarian control works partly by structuring traces, not only by changing beliefs. Once we distinguish low-risk consumption from low-visibility approval and from socially legible endorsement, the political meaning of a click changes. That insight extends preference falsification theory to platform-mediated behavior and suggests a more cautious reading of engagement metrics in authoritarian settings.

\section{WeChat, Public Accounts, and Interface Reforms \label{app:wechat-context}}

\subsection{Platform Scale and Official-Account Infrastructure}

Weixin and its international sister product WeChat are not merely messaging applications. They are a broad digital infrastructure that combines chat, social networking, public content distribution, payments, mini programs, and search within the same app environment. Tencent's 2024 annual results report 1.385 billion combined monthly active accounts as of 31 December 2024, while Tencent's product materials describe the platform as an ecosystem spanning Moments, Official Accounts, Weixin Pay, Mini Programs, Search, Channels, and related services \parencite{Tencent2025AnnualResults,TencentWeixinWeChatPage}. For this paper, the most important layer is the official-account system. Tencent's own description of Weixin Official Accounts presents it as a publishing and notification infrastructure through which individuals and organizations gather followers, push content, and send service or marketing notifications. The same page reports more than 20 million official accounts as of February 2021 and distinguishes between subscription accounts, oriented toward content publication and readership management, and service accounts, oriented toward user management and customer service \parencite{TencentOfficialAccountsPage}.

\subsection{Why Public Accounts Matter for Local Government}

This architecture matters for local-government communication because official accounts are embedded inside ordinary social and administrative life rather than separated into a standalone news app. A user may encounter a government article through a subscription feed, a chat share, a Moments post, a search result, or a recommendation channel such as \textit{kanyikan} (``See''). Once opened, the article sits inside the same environment that also contains private chats, group chats, payments, and service tools. That is why official accounts can function at once as broadcast channels, low-friction service interfaces, and distribution nodes inside the larger WeChat ecosystem. Tencent's 2017 first-quarter results, for example, already emphasized that official-account content was being tied more closely to search and mini-program traffic through embedded links and discovery tools \parencite{Tencent2017Q1Results}. Existing research on government WeChat accounts reaches a similar conclusion from the user side: many citizens treat these accounts as routine lookup and service tools rather than as purely political venues \parencite{JiangEtAl2021,LiuMaXia2024,YuShen2026}. For the purposes of this paper, the key implication is that article-level interaction takes place inside a platform where private communication, semi-public recommendation, and public-facing service use are tightly bundled together.

\subsection{Interface Reforms Relevant to Engagement Measures}

The article-level engagement measures used in this paper were shaped by a small number of platform redesigns that changed what users could do from the bottom of a public-account article page. First, contemporary reporting from late July 2014 documented that WeChat began displaying article-level read counts and a visible like button on public-account pages, creating the first widely visible article-side counters \parencite{People2014ReadLike}. Second, in May 2017, WeChat introduced ``Search'' and ``See'' through WeChat Lab and the discovery tab, broadening how users could encounter official-account content beyond simple subscription order \parencite{Sina2017SearchSee,Tencent2017Q1Results}. Third, on 21 December 2018, WeChat 7.0 replaced the older like button with \textit{haokan} (``good-looking''), and press coverage at the time emphasized that clicking \textit{haokan} could sync the article into the ``See'' feed and make the signal visible to friends \parencite{Zhoudao2018Haokan}. Fourth, on 15 March 2019, \textit{haokan} was renamed \textit{zaikan} (roughly, ``seen'' or ``reading now''); coverage framed the shift as softer wording for the same socially visible recommendation channel rather than as a new interaction type \parencite{TechNode2019Zaikan}. Finally, on 29 June 2020, the article footer was redesigned again: like returned alongside \textit{zaikan}, and a dedicated share button was added, making it easier to send articles to friends or Moments while also restoring a lower-visibility approval channel \parencite{STCN2020LikeReturn}.

Taken together, these reforms changed not only how official-account content circulated but also the social meaning of leaving a trace on an article page. In this paper's design, 2017 therefore serves mainly as a discovery and distribution reform, 2018 as a visibility-regime change in one-click approval, 2019 as a useful rename placebo, and late June 2020 as the return of a distinct lower-visibility like channel.

\subsection{Engagement Counters and Measurement Choices}

The article footer does not present a fixed menu of counters throughout the sample period. Before December 2018, the footer displayed a like button. From late December 2018 through June 2020, that slot became \textit{haokan}/\textit{zaikan}, which carried a more socially legible recommendation meaning because it linked to the ``See'' feed. From late June 2020 onward, the footer displayed both like and \textit{zaikan}, and also added a dedicated share button. Collect remained accessible through the top-right menu rather than the footer, which is why it serves as a useful placebo outcome in the main text.

These interface shifts motivate the paper's outcome construction. The main 2018 and 2020 outcome is therefore the \emph{one-click engagement rate}, defined as like plus \textit{haokan}/\textit{zaikan} whenever those channels occupy the same approval slot across regimes. That aggregation allows the design to separate changes in total approval from changes in how approval is allocated between lower-visibility and higher-visibility channels after 2020. Read counts require one additional harmonization step because WeChat top-codes some counters at ``100k+''; throughout the paper I recode those observations to 100,001 so they remain ordered while preserving the censoring boundary.

\section{WeChat Title Data and Classification Validation \label{app:wechat}}

This section documents the title-based WeChat classification pipeline used in the main analyses. The cleaned corpus covers all posts published by 329 prefecture-level government WeChat public accounts between 1 January 2015 and 31 December 2024. The key measurement constraint is that the archived source file preserves post titles consistently across the full nationwide collection but does not preserve article bodies in the current working archive. The unit of text classification is therefore the individual post title. In addition to the title itself, the working archive preserves auxiliary text-side cues such as a short keyword field, a short reason field, and a confidence score. These metadata are used only as classification inputs. Engagement outcomes such as reads, likes, shares, and \textit{zaikan} never enter the classification prompt.

\subsection{Human Coding Rules and Validation Sample \label{app:wechat-human}}

The validation audit drew 2,000 titles with sampling probabilities proportional to city-level corpus size, while retaining at least one title from every city in the corpus. This preserves exposure to the largest posting environments without collapsing the validation sample into only a handful of high-volume cities. Table~\ref{tab:wechat-annotation-sample-summary} summarizes the resulting audit sample.

\input{../tables/wechat_annotation_sample_summary.tex}

Two independent coders then assigned each title one and only one primary label and the corresponding higher-order family. The operative rule was to code the title's \emph{main communicative purpose}, not the broadest noun phrase or the most politically salient word in isolation. In practice, this meant preferring public-service information over welfare only when the title mainly explained how to do something; preferring leadership and political events over policy disclosure when leaders, meetings, inspections, speeches, or appointments were the center of the title; separating policy disclosure from governance and enforcement by distinguishing rule release from rule enforcement; and separating development performance from city-image content by distinguishing projects and economic upgrading from tourism, festivals, and branding. Coders were blind to model output and to all engagement outcomes. Disagreements were adjudicated to produce the final gold-standard label. The 2,000-title audit sample was held out from prompt design and used only for validation.

The March 2019 date ($p = 0.035$) falls near the haokan-to-zaikan rename, consistent with a small behavioral effect of the wording change.

The function of this table is to show that the main 2018 result is not easy to reproduce at arbitrary nearby dates. That matters because it pushes against purely seasonal or slowly evolving explanations.

Table~\ref{tab:placebo-2019-rename} formally tests the March 15, 2019 rename as a placebo: the one-click rate shows a modest positive shift ($p = 0.045$), while share and reads are unaffected. This supports the interpretation that the rename slightly reduced social pressure but did not constitute a fundamental visibility change.

Its function is interpretive cleanup. The table distinguishes a modest wording effect from the much larger redesign effects that altered the visibility structure of engagement.

\subsection{2017 Discovery Reform as a Contrast Case}

The function of this contrast table is conceptual. It shows that not every WeChat interface reform belongs in the same theoretical bucket: discovery changes look different from approval-visibility changes.
