  \begin{abstract}
  \noindent How do citizens in autocracies interact with official social media, and what do those interactions actually mean? Existing accounts often read engagement with state media either as evidence of propaganda success or as noise generated by skeptical audiences. I argue instead that official accounts are mixed channels that bundle public service information with political messaging, and that citizens sort their responses according to the visibility of the expressive act. Using 4.3 million posts from Chinese local government WeChat accounts, I first show that public service content dominates citizen attention, creating an attention dilemma: service information helps governments build durable audiences for future political communication while crowding out attention to overt propaganda at the moment of consumption. I then exploit platform reforms that changed whether approval was low-visibility or socially visible. A regression discontinuity design shows that visible endorsement of political content, especially hard propaganda, falls sharply when approval becomes socially legible, whereas reading and other lower-risk informational uses remain stable. When a lower-visibility like channel returns, approval partially rebounds. These findings recast engagement metrics as strategic behavioral traces rather than direct measures of regime support and extend preference falsification theory to layered digital expression.

\vspace{0.2in}

\noindent \textbf{Keywords}: digital authoritarianism; propaganda; official social media; WeChat; preference falsification; state--society interaction.

 \end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

How do citizens in autocracies engage with official social media, and what do those traces actually reveal about political alignment under repression? As propaganda, public communication, and service delivery move onto digital platforms, state-controlled accounts accumulate vast numbers of reads, likes, and shares. These metrics are tempting to interpret as indicators of propaganda success or regime support. Yet in authoritarian settings, interacting with official content is rarely a purely expressive act. Citizens may engage because they need information, because a post is difficult to avoid, because approval is low-cost, or because visible non-engagement is itself strategic. The political meaning of digital engagement is therefore fundamentally ambiguous.

Existing work speaks to parts of this puzzle, but rarely to the full mechanism at stake here. One literature on Chinese propaganda and media control shows that the party-state does not rely on crude indoctrination alone. It competes for attention through commercialization, soft propaganda, popularization, distraction, and clickbait, while audiences remain selective rather than passive \parencite{Brady2008,KingPanRoberts2017,LuPan2021,MattinglyYao2022,Roberts2018,Stockmann2013,StockmannGallagher2011,Wang2024Propaganda,Zou2021}. A second literature on preference falsification, self-censorship, and expressive caution emphasizes the gap between private belief and public expression under pressure \parencite{CarterCarterSchick2024,JiangYang2016,Kuran1995,NoelleNeumann1993,ShenTruex2020}. A third shows that government WeChat accounts are not merely propaganda outlets: local authorities also use them for public service delivery, routine contact, and audience acquisition \parencite{JiangEtAl2021,LiuMaXia2024,YuShen2026,Zheng2013,ZhengZheng2014}. What these literatures do not yet explain well is how platform design changes the political meaning of interaction itself.

I argue that official social media in digital authoritarianism are mixed channels that bundle public service information with political messaging, and that citizens sort their behavior across different levels of expressive risk. Service posts help governments attract followers, normalize repeated contact, and build durable audiences. Citizens, however, do not respond to all content, or to all buttons, in the same way. They distinguish between low-risk consumption, low-visibility approval, and socially visible endorsement. When a platform redesign makes endorsement more legible to peers, citizens should pull back first from the most politically loaded and most visible forms of engagement while preserving routine informational use \parencite{Brady2008,Kuran1995,LuPan2021,NoelleNeumann1993,Shen2025PSRM,ShenTruex2020,TsaiMen2018,Xu2022WeChat,YuShen2026}.

This perspective shifts the theoretical focus from whether citizens ``believe'' propaganda to how they manage observable traces of alignment. It also separates two behaviors that are often collapsed in studies of official media: consuming state content and publicly endorsing it. Citizens may continue to read official posts while avoiding visible signs of approval, especially when those signs are easily interpreted by friends, colleagues, and acquaintances. Preference falsification in digital authoritarianism is therefore not a binary gap between inner belief and public speech, but a layered behavioral process shaped by platform design and audience visibility \parencite{CarterCarterSchick2024,JiangYang2016,Karpa2026,Kuran1995,ShenTruex2020}.

I evaluate this argument using a new dataset of 4.3 million posts published by 329 Chinese local government WeChat official accounts between 2013 and early 2025. I classify posts by content type, distinguish hard from soft propaganda, and exploit platform reforms that changed whether approval was low-visibility or socially visible. Because these reforms altered the meaning of a click while leaving the underlying accounts in place, they provide unusually clean leverage for studying how interface design changes the political meaning of engagement.

The evidence reveals a consistent pattern. Public service content dominates citizen attention, creating an attention dilemma: it helps governments build future audiences for political communication while crowding out attention to overtly political content at the moment of consumption. When visible endorsement becomes more public, engagement with political content declines sharply, especially for hard propaganda, while reading and other lower-risk informational uses remain stable. When a lower-visibility like channel is restored, approval partially rebounds. These findings show that official engagement metrics are strategic behavioral traces rather than transparent measures of regime support.

This article makes three contributions. First, it extends preference falsification theory from speech to platform-mediated behavior by showing that expression is layered across reading, liking, \textit{zaikan}, and sharing. Second, it contributes to the study of propaganda and media consumption by separating exposure from endorsement and by showing how service content both builds and competes for political attention. Third, it recasts official social media as an everyday site of authoritarian state--society interaction, where governments manage not only information but also the terms under which citizens can safely engage.

\section{Theory}

Official social media in authoritarian settings should be understood as a platformed interaction regime rather than as a simple channel of state persuasion. Three literatures motivate this claim. First, preference falsification and self-censorship research explains why authoritarian citizens may mask preferences, avoid observable political expression, or strategically remain silent under pressure \parencite{CarterCarterSchick2024,JiangYang2016,Karpa2026,Kuran1995,ShenTruex2020}. Adjacent work on the spiral of silence highlights the social costs of signaling in front of relevant audiences \parencite{NoelleNeumann1993}. Second, research on Chinese propaganda and media control shows that the party-state does not merely transmit ideology; it manages information environments through credibility, entertainment, distraction, popularization, and agenda-setting \parencite{Brady2008,KingPanRoberts2017,LuPan2021,MattinglyYao2022,Roberts2018,Stockmann2013,Wang2024Propaganda,Zou2021}. Third, scholarship on government WeChat accounts shows that these are mixed institutional channels through which local authorities provide services, route transactions, manage crises, and sustain routine public contact \parencite{JiangEtAl2021,LiuMaXia2024,YuShen2026,Zheng2013,ZhengZheng2014}. What these strands leave underdeveloped is how platform design itself organizes the risk of political expression.

In classic preference falsification accounts, the central distinction is between private preference and public statement. On platforms such as WeChat, however, expression is not binary. The interface decomposes response into multiple actions---reading, liking, \textit{zaikan}, and sharing---that expose users to different audiences and therefore to different expressive risks. This matters because the relevant audience is dual. The state may observe aggregate traces, but friends, colleagues, and acquaintances can also infer meaning from visible acts. I therefore conceptualize digital preference falsification as layered expression: the same user may privately consume official content, register low-visibility approval, and avoid socially visible endorsement. This move extends work on self-censorship from speech and survey response to platform-mediated behavioral traces \parencite{Kuran1995,NoelleNeumann1993,ShenTruex2020,TsaiMen2018,Xu2022WeChat}.

This layered-expression logic changes how official propaganda should be studied. A large literature shows that Chinese propaganda is neither uniformly crude nor purely persuasive. It is often packaged in marketized, entertaining, emotionally resonant, and popularized forms, and it competes for attention in a crowded media environment \parencite{Brady2008,LuPan2021,MattinglyYao2022,StockmannGallagher2011,Wang2024Propaganda,Zou2021}. Citizens, meanwhile, are selective consumers rather than passive recipients \parencite{PanXu2018,Shen2025PSRM,WangWestwood2024}. The key implication for this paper is that exposure to official content and endorsement of official content are analytically distinct. A citizen may read a government post because it is useful, credible, or unavoidable without wishing to leave a visible trace of political alignment. Engagement metrics on official accounts therefore combine at least two processes: instrumental consumption and expressive positioning.

The distinction is especially important because Chinese government social media accounts are mixed channels rather than purely political outlets. Existing research shows that state agencies use social media not only to disseminate political messages but also to provide services, route users to transactions, manage crises, and sustain routine interaction with the public \parencite{JiangEtAl2021,LiuMaXia2024,YuShen2026,Zheng2013,ZhengZheng2014}. On WeChat official accounts in particular, service functions are embedded in the same interface as news and propaganda, and many users treat these accounts as practical lookup tools \parencite{JiangEtAl2021}. Recent work on local governments' WeChat communication strategies also underscores that public service information is not merely administratively residual content; it is itself part of how local states structure communication with citizens and cultivate support \parencite{YuShen2026}. This creates what I call an attention dilemma. Public service content helps governments build durable audiences, normalize repeated contact, and increase the probability that users encounter future political content. But at the moment of consumption, service content also competes with overt political messaging for scarce attention. Service information is thus politically consequential not because it is secretly propaganda in every instance, but because it helps sustain a channel through which the state can repeatedly reach citizens.

Finally, this framework recasts official social media as a site of everyday state--society interaction. Existing work on responsive authoritarianism, embedded control, and welfare provision shows that authoritarian durability depends not only on repression but also on routine responsiveness, selective benefits, and continuous low-intensity contact with society \parencite{Heurlin2016,Mattingly2020,Pan2020}. Official accounts extend this interaction into a platform environment where the state can simultaneously inform, serve, and observe, while citizens can respond at different levels of visibility. This framework yields three expectations. First, service content should attract substantially more consumption than overt propaganda because it provides immediate utility and lower expressive stakes. Second, when interface changes increase the visibility of endorsement, visible engagement with political posts should decline more than reading or other low-risk uses. Third, hard propaganda should be most sensitive to visibility because it sends the clearest and most polarizing signal; when a lower-visibility approval channel returns, rebound should occur there rather than uniformly across all expressive actions. Platform reforms, in short, do not merely change interface aesthetics. They reprice the political and social risks of interacting with the state.

\section{Contexts}

WeChat is the central digital infrastructure of everyday life in China. Since its launch in 2011, it has evolved into a multipurpose ``super app'' that combines messaging, payments, news distribution, and social networking within a single platform. By the 2020s, it had become the primary interface through which Chinese citizens communicate, consume news, and access digital public and private services. At the same time, content on the platform is subject to systematic deletion and filtering by Tencent and state censors, making WeChat a key site of both information control and regime legitimation \parencite{Ng2015,Roberts2020}. For a study of official digital communication in China, WeChat matters not because it is just another social media platform, but because it is deeply embedded in ordinary social, administrative, and economic life.

Within this ecosystem, ``public accounts'' (also called ``official accounts'') play a role similar to Facebook Pages or X accounts for organizations. Public accounts allow organizations to push articles to subscribers, provide customer service, and embed lightweight services within the app. The Chinese party-state is deeply embedded in this ecosystem. Central ministries, provincial and municipal governments, courts, police bureaus, and state media outlets all operate verified WeChat official accounts. These accounts are mixed institutional channels: they transmit propaganda and political messaging, publicize leadership priorities, and provide highly practical information and service announcements \parencite{JiangEtAl2021,LiuMaXia2024,YuShen2026,Zheng2013,ZhengZheng2014}. Official news feeds therefore combine political reports with practical advice, emergency guidance, and human-interest content, a strategy that makes propaganda harder to avoid and helps project competence and responsiveness \parencite{Brady2008,LuPan2021}.


A typical article on a public account appears as a mobile-optimized webpage with several built-in engagement mechanisms. The header displays the account name, publication time, and article title, followed by the main text, images, and any embedded video. At the bottom, WeChat presents a row of interactive buttons: a ``like'' (\textit{dianzan}) icon, a ``wow/seen'' button (\textit{zaikan}, previously branded \textit{haokan}), a ``share'' button that allows users to forward the article to individual chats or to their Moments feed,\footnote{Moments (\textit{pengyouquan}) is WeChat's semi-private social feed, analogous to Facebook's News Feed, where users share posts with their contacts.} a ``collect'' function (\textit{shoucang}) that bookmarks the article in a private folder, and a comment section (\textit{liuyan}) where followers can post and upvote responses. Each article also displays a cumulative read count, making overall attention to the content publicly visible. For this study, the key point is that these buttons do not simply measure the same thing with different labels. They ask users to leave different kinds of traces under different conditions of visibility.

Crucially for this study, WeChat has repeatedly redesigned the endorsement interface for public account articles in ways that change both the visibility and the implied cost of expressing approval for official news. In the early years of the public account platform, articles displayed a cumulative read count and a simple “like” (\textit{dianzan}) button at the bottom of the page. Users could tap to like an article, and only an aggregate like count was displayed; friends could not easily observe which specific items a user had endorsed. In this initial regime, signaling assent to official content was low-cost and largely private: visible engagement was essentially one-dimensional (read versus like), and likes did not visibly feed into a separate social discovery feed.

In May 2017, Tencent introduced the “See” (\textit{kan-yi-kan}) feature in version 6.5.8, initially through WeChat's experimental menu, adding a new discovery channel that aggregates popular and recommended content. “See” is a panel parallel to Moments (\textit{pengyouquan}) that displays a stream of articles surfaced by friends’ activity and algorithmic recommendations, rather than by direct following of accounts. The immediate implication of this 2017 change is therefore an exposure shock: official articles could, in principle, travel beyond the subscriber base and benefit from social proof, friend-based recommendation, and algorithmic amplification. At this stage, however, the primary endorsement control at the bottom of public account articles remained the simple like button. The core logic of endorsement was still that of a low-visibility, low-cost signal. For this reason, I treat the 2017 reform primarily as a discovery and audience-expansion shock, not as the cleanest discontinuity in the expressive cost of leaving a visible endorsement.

A first decisive discontinuity in the endorsement regime came on 21 December 2018, when WeChat 7.0 replaced the original like button on newly pushed public account articles with a “good-looking” (\textit{haokan}, later branded \textit{zaikan}) button tightly integrated with the “See” tab. Clicking “good-looking” no longer just incremented a counter at the bottom of the article; it also shared the article into the user’s “See” feed, where friends could view items that their contacts had marked as good-looking. In other words, a previously quasi-private like was transformed into a semi-public recommendation embedded in a social discovery stream. The implication is not merely a change in naming. After 21 December 2018, the same tap jointly performed two acts: it expressed approval and it recommended the article to one's social network. For official and political content, this reform therefore increased both the expected audience for a visible endorsement and the perceived reputational or political cost of leaving such a trace. Subsequent tweaks in early 2019 unified the labeling of this function under the name “seen” (\textit{zaikan}) across the bottom-of-article button and the “See” interface, but the core mechanism remained the same. I therefore treat the 2018 redesign as the paper's first primary discontinuity in visible endorsement cost.

A second major discontinuity occurred on 29 June 2020, when WeChat reintroduced the traditional like button at the bottom of public account articles while retaining the “seen” (\textit{zaikan}) button and adding a dedicated share button. After this redesign, the three actions—like, seen, and share—were displayed side by side in a horizontal row of recommended interactions. The new configuration created a layered menu of expression: users could register a low-visibility like that does not share the article into “See,” choose a semi-public \textit{zaikan} that continues to feed into the “See” tab, or engage in the most public form of amplification by sharing the article to chats or Moments. Importantly, the 2020 change was not a simple return to the pre-2018 world. Rather, it unbundled approval from recommendation and made the distinction among low-visibility approval, semi-public endorsement, and overt amplification explicit in the interface itself. In effect, the reform lowered the cost of one form of assent while preserving higher-visibility channels for socially observable support. I therefore treat the 2020 redesign as the paper's second primary discontinuity, this time capturing the restoration of a lower-risk approval channel within a layered expressive environment. Table~\ref{tab:policy_timepoints} summarizes these three interface shifts and the corresponding theoretical expectations. The 2017 reform is most useful for studying changes in discovery and reach, whereas the 2018 and 2020 reforms provide the cleaner quasi-exogenous discontinuities for the main analyses of expressive engagement.

\section{Research Design}

\subsection{Data}

This study uses the universe of posts published by 329 prefecture-level government WeChat public accounts between January 2013 and early January 2025, covering 31 provincial-level units and more than 4.3 million posts. The unit of analysis is the individual post. For each post, I record the account identifier, publication date, title, full article text, and all engagement indicators displayed in the WeChat interface, including read counts, likes, shares, comments, and the platform-specific \textit{haokan}/\textit{zaikan} counters where available. Because WeChat top-codes several counters at 100,000 (displayed as ``100k+''), I recode these values to 100,001 so they remain ordered while explicitly preserving censoring at the upper bound.\footnote{This recoding avoids missingness when engagement is analyzed as numeric. Results are robust to alternative treatments of top-coding (Appendix C).} Summary descriptive statistics for the corpus are reported in the appendix.

To categorize post content at scale, I use OpenAI's GPT-5.1 as a text coder rather than as a generator. The model receives the Chinese title and full article text, but not engagement outcomes, account identifiers, or indicators for the reform periods. The prompt instructs the model to identify the post's primary communicative purpose and return a structured JSON object containing a category label, a coarse confidence score, one to three decision keywords, and a brief justification.\footnote{I run the model with deterministic decoding (temperature = 0; other decoding parameters fixed) and set the output budget to accommodate the required JSON without truncation. The full prompt and all settings are provided in Appendix A.} Prompt development follows a two-stage procedure. I first draft a zero-shot prompt grounded in established typologies of Chinese official communication, then refine it on a separate random sample of 1,000 posts. After calibration, I freeze the prompt and apply it unchanged to the full corpus.\footnote{Appendix A provides the full classification documentation: the final prompt, decoding settings, category definitions, boundary-case rules, calibration procedure, label-standardization rules, and codebook mapping.}

I validate the LLM labels against an independently coded gold-standard sample. I randomly sample 3,000 posts from the corpus and recruit ten trained research assistants to label them using the same coding guide provided to the model. I treat the human labels as the reference standard and evaluate model performance using precision, recall, and F1 for multi-class classification. The appendix reports both the aggregate validation metrics and the full set of category-level results, including the confusion matrix.\footnote{Appendix B reports the full set of category-level precision, recall, and F1 statistics together with the confusion matrix used to diagnose classification error and motivate robustness checks.}

For the main analyses, I collapse the fine-grained labels into a small set of theoretically motivated content families. This aggregation is deliberate: the theory is about how different kinds of official content interact with different visibility costs, not about maximizing semantic granularity. Recent work on authoritarian messaging emphasizes that propaganda is not monolithic. Regimes pair overt, directive political communication with subtler, affectively engaging content designed to travel in mass and social media environments \parencite{KingPanRoberts2017,MattinglyYao2022,PanShaoXu2022,Wang2024Propaganda}. I therefore distinguish among hard propaganda, soft propaganda, and public service information.

Hard propaganda refers to politically explicit, heavy-handed communication that foregrounds hierarchy, discipline, ideology, and the state's coercive or organizational capacity. This form of messaging is often less about persuasion than about signaling regime strength and order, including through leader-centric communication and enforcement-oriented narratives \parencite{Brady2008,Wang2024Propaganda}. In the data, I code ``Leadership Activity and Official Governance'' and ``Regulation, Law Enforcement, and Discipline'' as hard propaganda.

Soft propaganda refers to regime-favorable communication packaged in relatively appealing, nonconfrontational, and socially legible forms---often emphasizing performance, everyday aspirations, and collective pride rather than direct political instruction. The literature suggests that these softer forms can persuade precisely because they are more entertaining, emotionally resonant, and less overtly didactic than hard propaganda \parencite{MattinglyYao2022,PanShaoXu2022}. In my data, ``Economic Development and Construction'' and ``Culture, Tourism, and City Branding'' fall into this soft-propaganda family because they associate the local state with competence, development, and positive affect.

Public service information is treated separately as instrumental governance content for everyday life. These posts primarily provide practical guidance---for example on service access, eligibility, procedures, weather, traffic, health, or emergencies---and are not principally aimed at overt political signaling, even though they are produced by state actors. This separation also aligns with research showing that official digital communication often mixes practical service delivery with less confrontational, non-argumentative political content \parencite{JiangEtAl2021,KingPanRoberts2017,LuPan2021}. Additional descriptive statistics and category-specific figures are reported in the appendix.

\subsection{Empirical Methods}
The first set of outcomes of interest concerns what content citizens consume from official social media and how governments allocate media production across content types. I therefore distinguish analytically between reforms that change exposure and reforms that change the meaning of an expressive act. The May 2017 introduction of ``See'' is used primarily to study discovery, audience expansion, and possible changes in reading and diffusion, since the bottom-of-article like button itself remained in place and the feature initially rolled out through WeChat's experimental menu. By contrast, the 21 December 2018 and 29 June 2020 redesigns directly altered what a click at the bottom of a public-account article meant. The 2018 reform bundled approval with social recommendation and raised the visibility cost of endorsement; the 2020 reform unbundled approval from recommendation and restored a lower-visibility approval channel while preserving more public ones. These latter two moments therefore serve as the main discontinuities for the paper's regression-discontinuity analyses of expressive engagement, while the 2017 change is used more cautiously as an exposure-side reform.
